Let's talk about one of the more genuinely useful tools in an investor's kit: the 1031 exchange. Sell an investment property, roll the proceeds into another one, and the capital gains tax you'd normally owe gets deferred instead of due. Do this enough times, strategically, and you can keep deferring it indefinitely. There's even a name for the endgame, and we'll get to that, because it's a little dark and I love it.

**A 1031 exchange lets an investor sell business or investment real estate and defer the capital gains tax by reinvesting the proceeds into a like-kind replacement property, provided a qualified intermediary holds the funds, the investor identifies replacement property within 45 days of closing, and completes the purchase within 180 days.**

That's the official version. Here's the more useful version: the IRS built a legitimate, well-worn path for investors to keep growing their portfolio without everyone's least favorite uncle (Sam) taking a cut at every single step. It just comes with a strict rulebook, so let's walk through it.

## What Actually Qualifies as "Like-Kind"

This is more forgiving than most people assume. Since a 2017 change to the tax code, only real property qualifies, personal property like equipment or vehicles no longer does. But among real estate, "like-kind" is interpreted broadly. A duplex can exchange into a multifamily building across the country. A commercial storefront can exchange into raw land held for investment. What matters is that both properties are held for investment or business use, not personal use. Your primary residence doesn't qualify, and neither does a vacation home you actually enjoy using yourself, unless it's been run as a genuine rental with limited personal use.

## The Timeline (Set Your Reminders Now)

Once your relinquished property closes, two clocks start running at the same time, not back to back:

- **45 days** to formally identify potential replacement properties in writing, delivered to your qualified intermediary
- **180 days** total to close on the replacement property, or the due date of your tax return for that year, whichever comes first

Both are calendar days, weekends and holidays included. The one exception is an IRS-declared disaster postponement. Close late in the year, say October or later, and your 180-day window can get shortened by your tax filing deadline unless you file an extension.

For identifying replacement properties, you get to pick your approach:

- **The three-property rule**, the crowd favorite: identify up to three potential properties, regardless of their combined value
- **The 200% rule**: identify any number of properties, as long as their combined value doesn't exceed 200% of what you sold
- **The 95% rule**: identify any number of properties at any value, but then you actually have to close on at least 95% of that combined value

Most investors go with the three-property rule. It's simple, and it gives you backups if your first choice falls through.

## The Qualified Intermediary Isn't Optional

You cannot touch the sale proceeds at any point, not even briefly, or the exchange falls apart. A qualified intermediary holds the funds between the sale and the purchase, and it has to be a genuinely neutral third party. Your real estate agent, your CPA, your attorney, or a family member cannot serve as your QI if they've worked for you in that capacity in the past two years. Get this lined up before you close on the property you're selling, not after, since your QI needs to already be in place at closing.

## What Can Still Trigger a Tax Bill

A 1031 exchange defers tax, but it's not a blanket pass if you're not paying attention. Two things create a taxable event even inside an otherwise valid exchange:

- **Boot**: any cash or non-like-kind value you walk away with instead of reinvesting. Sell for more than you spend on the replacement and pocket the difference, and that difference gets taxed.
- **Debt reduction**: if your replacement property carries a smaller mortgage than the one you sold, the IRS treats that gap the same as cash boot, even if you never actually saw the money.

The simple version: to fully defer the gain, reinvest all your proceeds and take on equal or greater debt on the replacement.

## California's Extra Layer: The Clawback

California generally goes along with the federal 1031 rules, but it doesn't let go quite as easily as the IRS does. If you exchange California property into a replacement property outside the state, the Franchise Tax Board tracks that deferred gain through an annual filing, **Form FTB 3840**. Whenever you eventually sell that out-of-state property in a taxable transaction, even decades later and even if you've long since moved away yourself, California taxes the original deferred gain like it never left. This is the California clawback, and it's an ongoing annual filing obligation, not a one-and-done form.

There's also a mechanical detail at closing worth knowing: California generally withholds 3 1/3% of the sale price on real estate transactions unless you claim an exemption. For a 1031 exchange, you claim that exemption on Form 593 by checking the box certifying the sale is part of a like-kind exchange. Get it right and your full proceeds move to the qualified intermediary. Miss it, and a real chunk of your exchange funds sits with the state instead of working for you. A very important literal box to check.

## Does a DST Solve Any of This?

We touched on Delaware Statutory Trusts in [the Prop 19 post](/blog/prop-19-parent-child-property-tax-transfer-california), and they come up constantly alongside 1031 exchanges specifically. A DST is a legal entity that holds title to investment real estate, and fractional ownership interests in that trust qualify as like-kind replacement property under Section 1031. That means you can exchange a property you've managed yourself for years into a passive, professionally managed interest in something much larger, an apartment complex, a medical office building, whatever the DST holds, without losing your tax deferral.

This is popular with investors who are done fielding maintenance calls but aren't ready to cash out and pay the tax bill. Minimum investments typically start somewhere in the $100,000 to $250,000 range.

## Okay, Let's Talk About "Swap 'Til You Drop"

Trigger warning: we're about to go a little dark for a real estate blog. (Especially if you thought you were coming here to <a href="https://caila.lametrohomefinder.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">peep some pretty houses</a>.)

"Swap 'til you drop" is the actual, real, widely used term investors and CPAs use for a strategy where you just keep doing 1031 exchanges, one after another, for the rest of your life. Sell, exchange, defer. Sell again, exchange again, defer again. You never cash out, so you never trigger the tax. And then, well, you drop, meaning you pass away still holding the replacement property.

(It's like shop 'til you drop, but instead of settling in at Sbarro after a marathon mall run, you end up in that beautiful food court in the sky. Probably. IDK, we're getting a little too philosophical for real estate. I digress.)

Here's the part that makes this whole strategy actually work: when you die still holding that property, your heirs receive a stepped-up cost basis, reset to the property's value at the time of your death. All of that capital gains tax you'd been deferring for years or decades? It doesn't transfer to your heirs. It just disappears. They can sell the property shortly after inheriting it and owe little to no capital gains tax at all, even though you personally deferred a mountain of it the whole time you owned it.

Is that a little morbid as an investment strategy? Sure, a bit. Is it also a completely legitimate, extremely popular estate planning tool among real estate investors? Also yes. It's the reason so many long-term investors treat 1031 exchanges less like a one-time tax trick and more like a permanent operating strategy, sell, upgrade, defer, repeat, until it becomes someone else's property tax problem and someone else's tax-free windfall. Fair warning, if the plan is to eventually pass that property to your kids, Prop 19 still fully reassesses investment property for property tax purposes no matter what happens on the capital gains side, so this strategy solves one tax problem, not both.

## A Word on Cap Rates, Since This Is Still an Investment Decision

Before exchanging into anything, actually run the numbers instead of chasing a bigger building for its own sake. Cap rate, short for capitalization rate, is a quick way to compare income properties: take the property's annual net operating income and divide it by its purchase price. A $1,000,000 property generating $60,000 in net income a year has a 6% cap rate. Lower cap rates generally mean a pricier, often lower-risk property in a stronger market. Higher cap rates usually mean more yield, but often more risk or more management.

A 1031 exchange is a tax strategy, not an investment strategy on its own. Trading a well-performing rental for a property with a worse cap rate just to defer taxes can leave you with more headaches and less income than you started with. Run this math with your CPA and a lender before you fall for a replacement property.

## When a 1031 Exchange Strategy Makes Sense

A few scenarios where a 1031 exchange genuinely earns its keep:

- **Trading up** from a smaller rental into a larger property to increase cash flow and diversify tenant risk
- **Consolidating** several scattered rentals into one larger, easier to manage property
- **Relocating capital** into a market with stronger rent growth, keeping in mind California's clawback still applies if you leave the state
- **Going passive** through a DST when active management no longer fits your life
- **Playing the long game**, combining repeated exchanges with an eventual step-up in basis, ideally with an estate attorney in the loop about how it interacts with Prop 19 if kids are eventually inheriting the property

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a 1031 exchange?

A 1031 exchange is a strategy under Section 1031 of the Internal Revenue Code that allows an investor to sell business or investment real estate and defer capital gains tax by reinvesting the proceeds into a like-kind replacement property, following strict timing and reinvestment rules.

### How long do I have to complete a 1031 exchange?

You have 45 calendar days from the closing of your relinquished property to identify potential replacement properties in writing, and 180 calendar days total to close on the replacement, or the due date of your tax return for that year, whichever comes first. Both deadlines run concurrently.

### What does "swap 'til you drop" mean?

"Swap 'til you drop" refers to repeatedly using 1031 exchanges to defer capital gains tax on investment property for the rest of an investor's life, without ever cashing out. When the investor passes away still holding the property, their heirs receive a stepped-up cost basis, which can eliminate the deferred capital gains tax entirely.

### What is the California clawback on a 1031 exchange?

If you exchange California investment property into a replacement property located outside California, the Franchise Tax Board tracks the deferred gain through an annual Form FTB 3840 filing. When the out-of-state replacement property is eventually sold in a taxable transaction, California taxes that original deferred gain, regardless of where you live at the time.

### Can a Delaware Statutory Trust (DST) be used in a 1031 exchange?

Yes. Fractional ownership interests in a DST qualify as like-kind replacement property under Section 1031, allowing an investor to exchange out of actively managed property into a passive, professionally managed interest without losing the tax deferral. It's also a strong backup plan if you're nearing that 45-day deadline and haven't found a property worth acquiring.

## The Bottom Line

A 1031 exchange can preserve a significant amount of equity when you're ready to sell an investment property, and for the right investor, it can turn into a lifelong strategy rather than a one-time move. It just runs on the IRS's clock, not yours, and California adds its own reporting requirements on top of the federal rules. Start the planning before you list the property, with a qualified intermediary already lined up, not after you've accepted an offer.
