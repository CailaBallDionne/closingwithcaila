If you are fortunate enough to have a family home inheritance in your future, you may have grown up assuming the home would just pass to you someday at your parents' old tax bill. Welp, I have some news. While that assumption was true for decades, it has not been true since February 2021.

Prop 19 rewrote the rules for transferring property between parents and children in California, and it did it in a way that catches a lot of families off guard, usually at the worst possible time, right when they're also dealing with a parent's estate.

**Prop 19 replaced California's old parent-child property tax exclusion with a much narrower one: it only protects a home that the child actually moves into as their primary residence within a year of the transfer, and only up to a set dollar amount above the parent's existing assessed value.**

Have you read that sentence 5 times trying to figure out what it means for you? Yeah, Prop 19 follows that life rule of "nothing is easy." So, let's break down the many conditions packed into Prop 19.

## What It Used to Be, Before Prop 19

Under the old rules (Prop 58 and Prop 193, if you want the technical names), a parent could hand down their primary residence to a child, plus up to $1 million of assessed value in other property, like a rental or a second home, and none of it got reassessed. The child could keep it as a rental, live in it, sell it eventually, didn't matter. The tax basis rode along with the property no matter what the child did with it.

That's gone. Prop 19 closed that door on everything except the family home, and even that one comes with strings attached.

## What Still Qualifies (And the Strings Attached)

For a parent-child transfer to keep the parent's tax basis today, all of these have to be true:

- The property has to have been the parent's principal residence
- The child has to move in and make it their own primary residence within one year of the transfer
- The child has to file for the homeowner's exemption (or disabled veteran's exemption) on that property within one year of the transfer
- The child has to file a claim for the exclusion itself, form BOE-19-P, with the county assessor, generally within three years of the transfer

Miss any one of these and the exclusion either doesn't apply or only kicks in from whenever you eventually get around to filing, not from the original transfer date. This is not a "figure it out later" situation. The clock starts the day the transfer happens.

## What No Longer Qualifies At All

This is the part that surprises people the most. Under the old rules, a parent could also transfer up to $1 million in other real property, a rental unit, a vacation place, a second home, and it kept its tax basis regardless of what the child did with it.

Under Prop 19, that's gone. Rental properties, vacation homes, and investment properties get reassessed to full current market value the moment title changes hands between parent and child, full stop. There's no partial break, no dollar threshold, nothing. If a family's plan involves passing down anything other than the actual primary residence, that property is getting reassessed at today's value, and the tax bill is going to reflect it.

## The Dollar Math (Because It's Not Unlimited)

Even for a qualifying primary residence, the exclusion isn't unlimited. Here's how it actually works: the new taxable value is the parent's existing assessed value, plus an exclusion amount that adjusts every two years for inflation. For transfers happening between February 16, 2025 and February 15, 2027, that exclusion amount is the super specific $1,044,586.

Say a parent's home has an assessed value of $300,000, but it's worth $1.5 million on the day it transfers to the child. Add the assessed value to the exclusion amount: $300,000 + $1,044,586 = $1,344,586. The home's market value exceeds that by $155,414, so that difference gets added to the parent's original assessed value. The child ends up with a new taxable value of $455,414, not the full $1.5 million, but not the parent's original $300,000 either. Somewhere in between.

If the home's market value had come in under that combined threshold, the child would have kept the parent's exact assessed value with zero adjustment. The gap between assessed value and market value is what determines whether you get the full break, a partial one, or none at all.

## Does Putting the Home in a Trust Help?

This comes up in almost every conversation I have about Prop 19, so let's address it directly: no, not in the way most people hope.

A revocable living trust does not avoid Prop 19 reassessment. The trust is a mechanism for how a property passes, it helps you skip probate, keep things private, and control the terms of distribution, but it doesn't change what happens when the property actually transfers from parent to child. Whether that transfer happens through a trust, a will, or without either one, the same Prop 19 rules apply: it has to be the parent's primary residence, the child has to move in within a year, and the paperwork has to get filed on time. Putting the house in a trust doesn't create some separate, more favorable set of rules. The county assessor cares about the change in beneficial ownership, not which legal document made it happen.

Where a trust does earn its keep is in the details that trip families up. If more than one child is named as a beneficiary, a vague or outdated trust can leave a county assessor with no clear answer about who's actually supposed to live in the home and claim the exclusion, and a default assumption that nobody qualifies is exactly how families end up reassessed by accident. A trust that's been updated with Prop 19 in mind can explicitly name the intended occupant, spell out the trustee's responsibility to notify beneficiaries of the filing deadlines, and reduce the odds that this gets missed during what's already a difficult time. The trust isn't a tax shield, but it can be the thing that keeps the exclusion from slipping through the cracks on a technicality.

## Other Ways Families Plan Ahead of This

A trust isn't the only tool in the box. A few others that come up, all of which need a real attorney involved, not a DIY approach:

- **Legal entity structuring for investment property.** This one's specific to rentals and non-primary-residence property, which get zero exclusion under Prop 19 no matter what. Some families transfer that kind of property into an LLC while the parent is still alive, then gradually transfer membership interests to the kids over time, structured to avoid triggering a full "change in control" under California's Revenue and Taxation Code. Done correctly and early, this can preserve the tax basis on property that would otherwise get fully reassessed. Done incorrectly or after the fact, it does nothing and can create its own problems. This needs years of lead time and a specialist, not a conversation that starts after a parent has passed.
- **Naming one clear occupant in advance**, rather than leaving multiple siblings to sort it out informally. The exclusion only needs one qualifying child living there, but the assessor needs that to be documented and unambiguous, not assumed.
- **Running the numbers before anything transfers.** If a family knows roughly what the assessed value and current market value are, they can estimate whether they'll land under the exclusion cap, over it with a partial reassessment, or facing a full one, and plan around that reality instead of finding out after the fact.
- **1031 exchanges into a DST (Delaware Statutory Trust) for investment property.** This one solves a different problem entirely, capital gains, not property tax, so don't mix up the two. Prop 19 still fully reassesses investment property no matter what you do. But if a parent 1031 exchanges a rental into a DST and holds it until they pass, their heirs receive a stepped-up cost basis, which can wipe out the capital gains tax that would otherwise be owed on decades of appreciation. It won't touch the Prop 19 property tax reset, that still happens, but it can seriously soften the overall tax hit, especially when multiple heirs don't all want to be landlords and would rather split up a passive, professionally managed investment. Another one for a 1031 specialist (I've got one on speed dial) and CPA, not something to DIY.

None of these are things I can advise on directly. But knowing they exist means you walk into the estate attorney's office with better questions instead of finding out about your options for the first time after a parent has already passed.

## What This Looks Like in La Crescenta and La Cañada Flintridge

This is where the conversation gets real for a lot of my clients. The Foothills have a lot of long-held family homes, places bought decades ago that are now worth many multiples of their assessed value, and a lot of multigenerational households where the plan has always been "the kids will take over the house."

That plan still works, but it now depends entirely on whether a child is actually going to live there. If one sibling wants to move in and the others don't, the exclusion can still apply as long as one eligible child occupies it as a primary residence, files the homeowner's exemption, and the paperwork gets filed on time. But if the family's real plan is to keep the home as a rental for extra income, or split it and use it as a vacation property, none of that qualifies anymore, and the reassessment can be a genuine shock given how much these properties have appreciated.

I'd rather a family have this conversation with an accountant or estate attorney years before a transfer actually happens than find out about the dollar threshold the week they're also grieving a parent and settling an estate. This is squarely outside my lane as an agent, but it's exactly the kind of thing worth building into an estate plan alongside a trust and a will.

## Worth Watching: There's a Repeal Effort in Motion

A signature-gathering effort to repeal the inheritance portions of Prop 19 has been circulating since late 2025, aiming for the November 2026 ballot. Two earlier attempts didn't qualify. If this one does and voters approve it, the old Prop 58/193 rules would come back, meaning children could inherit a family home at any value without reassessment and the $1 million exclusion for other property would return too. Nothing has changed yet, and I wouldn't plan around a repeal that hasn't happened, but it's worth keeping an eye on if you're mapping out a transfer timeline.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does Prop 19 apply to rental or investment property transferred between parents and children?

No. Rental properties, vacation homes, and investment properties receive no exclusion under Prop 19 and are reassessed to full market value when title transfers between parent and child. Only a home the child moves into as their primary residence within one year can qualify.

### What is the current Prop 19 exclusion amount?

For transfers occurring between February 16, 2025 and February 15, 2027, the exclusion amount is $1,044,586 above the parent's existing assessed value. This figure is adjusted every two years based on California home price data, so it will be different outside that window.

### Does putting a home in a trust avoid Prop 19 reassessment?

No. A revocable living trust does not avoid Prop 19 reassessment on its own. It controls how a property passes and helps avoid probate, but the same Prop 19 rules apply once the property actually transfers to a child, whether that happens through a trust, a will, or otherwise. A well-drafted trust can help by clearly naming which beneficiary is meant to occupy the home, which reduces the risk of losing the exclusion due to confusion when multiple children are named.

### Do all the children have to move into the inherited home?

No. If multiple children inherit the family home, only one needs to move in and use it as their primary residence for the exclusion to apply. If that child later moves out, another sibling can move in within one year and the exclusion continues, as long as that sibling also files for the homeowner's exemption.

### What happens if we miss the one-year deadline to move in?

If the child doesn't move in and file for the homeowner's or disabled veteran's exemption within one year of the transfer, the property is reassessed at full market value as of the transfer date. There is no exclusion for a home used as a rental or second home after inheriting it.

### Does a 1031 exchange or DST help with Prop 19?

Not directly. A 1031 exchange and Delaware Statutory Trust deal with federal and state capital gains tax, a completely separate system from Prop 19's property tax reassessment. Investment property still gets reassessed to full market value under Prop 19 regardless. But holding investment property in a DST until death lets heirs receive a stepped-up cost basis, which can reduce or eliminate the deferred capital gains tax, even though the property tax basis still resets.

## The Bottom Line

Prop 19 didn't just adjust the parent-child transfer rules, it narrowed them to one specific scenario: a child who actually moves in. Everything else, rentals, vacation homes, keeping the place as an investment, gets reassessed at today's value. If your family has a home you're planning to pass down, this is worth a real conversation with your estate attorney or CPA well before it becomes urgent, not after.
